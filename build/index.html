The Western Lake Erie Restoration Assessment (WLERA) model was co-developed by investigators in the Environmental Studies Program at the New College of Florida in Sarasota, Florida and the USGS - Great Lakes Science Center in Ann Arbor, Michigan. The web mapping application for interfacing with the WLERA model was developed by programmers at the Wisconsin Internet Mapping Group.

The WLERA is a geodesign framework for measuring the potential to restore coastal wetlands in the Great Lakes basin. This framework is based on the expert coupling of multiple criteria decision analysis and geographic information systems. Experts included regional wetland ecologists, biologists, planners and geographers from federal, state, academic and non- governmental organizations. Criteria include historical water levels, topography, soils, mapped wetlands, managed lands, and impervious and non-impervious development on the landscape. The WLERA data and kernel analysis are organized in ArcGIS 10.3 geodatabases and python applications.

Within the 195,621 ha study area between the mouths of the Detroit River, MI and Black River, OH, the WLERA normalized composite index model identified over 7,500 hectares of areas highly suitable for coastal wetland habitat restoration. The model results 1) range from -100 to 100 with positive values representing areas with higher potential for restoration (yellow to green). 2) have a pyramided spatial resolution of 1 meter at the lowest level, and 3) can be aggregated by any irregular area, including individual land ownership parcel boundaries or custom boundaries created with the Area Summary Tool in the web mapping application. Areas with the highest restorability class (greater than 80) coincided with 10 areas currently selected by GLRI for restoration in WLE. As the region continues to restore coastal wetland habitats, this tool and analysis will support restoration decisions.

The development of the WLERA has been supported by the Great Lakes Restoration Initiative, the Upper Midwest and Great Lakes Landscape Conservation Cooperative and the University of Michigan Water Center.



Below is a list of WLERA layers with description and source. For complete metadata, please visit the USGS ScienceBase catalog item for GLCWRA

WLERA Composite Index: Restorability model developed by New College of Florida, USGS, TNC, and NOAA using detailed terrain and historical lake levels, and national soils, wetlands, land use, and land cover data.
Source: New College of Florida, USGS

Study Area Boundary: The boundary line for the WLERA study area was determined with the alongshore extent from the Detroit River in Michigan to the Black River in Ohio. The offshore boundary is determined with a 2 km buffer from the mapped coastline. The landward boundary is determined from the elevation of the maximum Western Lake Erie lake water level height buffered by 1 m of elevation and 1 km inland. For informational purposes only.
Source: New College of Florida and U.S. Geological Survey

Land Parcels: The parcels were collected from the tax collector and property appraisers departments for respective counties. The parcel boundaries represent the geometry published by each county in September 2015. For informational purposes only.
Source: Monroe County, Michigan; Lucas County, Ottawa County, Sandusky County, Erie County, Lorain County, Ohio.

Oblique Aerial Imagery: These points show the locations of linked ACOE oblique aerial photography from greatlakes.usace.army.mil
Source: US Army Corps of Engineers

Lake Level Stations: Locations of lake level measurements from NOAA CO-OPS.
Source: NOAA CO-OPS

Wetland Biological Integrity: Plant indicator scores are based on data collected as part of the Great Lakes Coastal Wetland Monitoring Program. Total vegetation indicator scores are based on 10 metrics that collectively evaluate the relative condition of each wetland. Metrics included entire-site and vegetation-zone specific calculations, which are combined into an overall site score. Metric and scoring calculations are described at greatlakeswetlands.org
Source: Great Lakes Coastal Wetland Monitoring Program

GLRI Wetland Restoration Areas: Project boundaries digitized from GLRI habitat restoration database. http://habitat.glc.org/
Source: New College of Florida

Parameters Group
Water Mask (P0): Areas excluded from the analysis but within study area boundary. Most areas are permanent or perennial water features previously mapped by the National Wetlands Inventory and the National Hydrographic Dataset.
Source: New College of Florida, USGS

Hydroperiod (P1): Developed as the first parameter to the restorability model. Hydroperiod is the amount of time a coastal landscape area is inundated with surface water from western Lake Erie. Areas more frequently inundated are better for restoration.
Source: New College of Florida, USGS

Wetsoils (P2): Developed as the second parameter to the restorability model. Wetland soils areas are mapped as existing wetlands or as hydrologic soils that support wetlands. These areas are better for restoration.
Source: New College of Florida, USGS

Flowline (P3): Developed as the third parameter to the restorability model. Flowline areas are mapped as part of a drainage network connecting to Lake Erie. These areas are better for restoration.
Source: New College of Florida, USGS

Conserved Lands (P4): Developed as the fourth parameter to the restorability model representing areas within ranges of mapped CARL boundaries. Areas closer to CARL boundaries are better for restoration.
Source: New College of Florida, USGS

Impervious Surfaces (P5): Developed as the fifth parameter to the restorability model representing areas with impervious surfaces such as roads, parking lots and buildings. Impervious surfaces can be important societal infrastructure and expensive / difficult to modify for restoration.
Source: New College of Florida, USGS

Landuse (P6): Developed as the sixth parameter to the restorability model representing areas without impervious surfaces but high societal importance such as active agriculture, golf courses and parks. These areas can be expensive / difficult to modify for restoration.
Source: New College of Florida, USGS

Hydro Conditioning Group
Culverts: Locations of potential culverts or other hydraulic control structures. These locations are important for understanding the hydrologic connections between a coastal area and the lake.
Source: New College of Florida, University of Michigan-Dearborn, The Nature Conservancy, NOAA, USGS

Degree Flowlines: Attribute flowline reaches used in model parameter three with the number of artificial disconnections between the reach and western Lake Erie. These data will help identify the degree of hydrologic separation between potential restoration areas and Lake Erie.
Source: New College of Florida, University of Michigan-Dearborn, The Nature Conservancy, NOAA, USGS

Dikes: Modeled locations of dikes. Dikes are important control features for restoring coastal wetlands.
Source: New College of Florida, University of Michigan-Dearborn, The Nature Conservancy, NOAA, USGS
